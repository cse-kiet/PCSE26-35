| `trajectory_plot.png` | `make visualise` | Path of every vehicle through the intersection |

## `trajectory_plot.png`

**Generated by:** `visualise_traces.py` → `plot_trajectories()`

Shows the complete path of every vehicle through the simulation. Each vehicle is
a different colour. Circles mark the start position; squares mark the end position.
Dashed lines show the road centre axes.

**How to read it:**
- Paths that run along the horizontal axis are east-west vehicles
- Paths that run along the vertical axis are north-south vehicles
- Short paths belong to vehicles that entered late or exited early
- Clusters near (0, 300) or (300, 0) are vehicles queuing at the traffic light

**What to look for:**
- Are vehicles using all four arms, or are they all entering from one side?
  If routes are uneven, try `make sumo-rebuild SEED=99` for different patterns.
- Are there paths that pass through the intersection centre? These vehicles
  crossed without stopping — they had a green light.

## `heatmap.png`

**Generated by:** `visualise_traces.py` → `plot_heatmap()`

A 2D histogram of all vehicle positions across the entire simulation. Darker colour
= more time spent at that location. Uses a hot colourmap (black = maximum density,
yellow = moderate, white = rarely visited).

**How to read it:**
- Dark spots at the ends of arms are vehicle entry/exit queues
- Dark spots at the intersection centre are vehicles crossing or waiting
- The colour scale is relative density — even a "light" colour means many timesteps

**What to look for:**
- Is the intersection centre dark? If yes, vehicles are slowing down / stopping there.
  This is expected due to the traffic signal.
- Are all four arms active? If only two arms are lit up, the route generation was
  biassed toward one direction. Use `--rebuild` with a different seed.

## `speed_profile.png`

**Generated by:** `visualise_traces.py` → `plot_speed_profiles()`

Line plot of each vehicle's speed (m/s) over time. Each vehicle is a different colour.

**How to read it:**
- Speed = 0 means the vehicle is stopped (at a traffic light, or hasn't entered yet)
- Sudden drops to 0 followed by acceleration are traffic light stops
- The maximum speed is 13.89 m/s (50 km/h) — vehicles approaching the limit cruise
  on the open arm before they reach the intersection
- Short lines are vehicles that only appeared for part of the simulation

**What to look for:**
- The sawtooth pattern (accelerate → stop → accelerate) shows the traffic signal
  cycle is working correctly
- All vehicles should eventually reach near-maximum speed on open road
- If all vehicles stay at 0 speed, SUMO's routing may have failed

## Policy comparison bar chart

**Generated by:** `intersectionmultipolicy_sumo.m`

A grouped bar chart with one group per policy. Two bars per group:
- Blue bar — Completion Rate (fraction of tasks that were completed)
- Orange bar — Offloading Rate (fraction of tasks that were offloaded to another vehicle)

**How to read it:**
- A high completion rate is good — it means tasks are getting done before their deadlines
- A high offloading rate means the policy makes use of V2V offloading frequently
- A policy with high completion but low offloading (like SpeedCheck or Game-Theoretic)
  achieves reliability by being conservative — only offloading when it is clearly beneficial
- A policy with both high completion and high offloading (Load-Balancing) is ideal —
  it distributes work effectively and still meets deadlines

## Statistical error-bar plots (`run_trials.m`)

Two subplots side by side:
- Left: Completion Rate — bar heights are means, error bars are std deviations
- Right: Offloading Rate — same format

**How to read it:**
- Tall bars are good for completion rate
- Short error bars mean the policy behaves consistently regardless of random conditions
- Long error bars mean the policy's performance depends heavily on which vehicles
  happen to appear together — it is sensitive to the specific scenario

**For the paper:** Report mean ± std from this plot. "Policy X achieves completion
rate 0.XXX ± 0.XXX across 30 independent trials."

## Stress test sweep plots (`stress_test.m`)

Four subplots arranged as 2 rows × 2 columns:

**Top row — Density sweep (vehicle count on x-axis):**
- Left: Completion Rate vs number of vehicles
- Right: Offloading Rate vs number of vehicles

**Bottom row — Load sweep (task arrival rate on x-axis):**
- Left: Completion Rate vs arrival rate
- Right: Offloading Rate vs arrival rate

**How to read density sweep:**
- Rising completion rate with more vehicles means offloading helps — more partners
  available means more tasks can be distributed
- Falling completion rate with more vehicles means congestion — too many tasks,
  not enough processing capacity

**How to read load sweep:**
- All policies should show declining completion rate as arrival rate increases
  (more tasks = harder to keep up)
- The question is *how fast* the rate declines — a robust policy degrades slowly
- Offloading rate typically increases with load — under pressure, vehicles offload
  more aggressively

## SUMO GUI (`make sumo-gui`)

Opens the SUMO graphical interface with the intersection loaded and ready to play.

**Controls:**
| Action | How |
|---|---|
| Start simulation | Green play button or `Ctrl+A` |
| Pause | Pause button or `Ctrl+S` |
| Speed up | Move speed slider right |
| Slow down | Move speed slider left |
| Zoom in | Scroll wheel up |
| Zoom out | Scroll wheel down |
| Pan | Click and drag the canvas |
| Inspect a vehicle | Click on it |
| Step one frame | `Ctrl+D` (while paused) |

**What to observe:**
- Vehicles enter from one of the four arm endpoints and travel toward the centre
- Vehicles stop at the red traffic signal and wait
- When the signal turns green, the waiting queue accelerates through
- Vehicle colours change with speed by default (blue = slow, red = fast)
- Click a vehicle to see its ID, speed, position, and which edge it is on

**Note:** `make sumo-gui` exits immediately after the simulation ends (100 s).
To pause and inspect before the sim finishes, slow down the playback slider or
use step mode.
